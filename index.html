Analysis of e-Waste buildup

eCommerce purchase history datasets from Kaggle and data crawled from product descriptions have been used for this analysis.

Julia source code for this analysis is available as Open Source here : eWaste analysis source code

Insights:

  • Smartphones is the largest category by volume and value however, TVs would generate more than 238,000 Kg of ewaste - A massive 45%
  • Smartphones' batteries are usually Li-ion, manufacturing of which has substantial environment impact
  • All the products that were sold will eventually translate to e-waste after they become obsolete.
  • Assuming that these products aren't refurbished, within one year of the sales, 396 kg of ewaste in generated.
  • This number grows 100% every year for the next 5 years, reaching a staggering 73 tonnes.
  • In just 15 years, a monstrous 531,256 kg of e-waste would have been generated. Unfortunately, much of this waste is exported and ends up in vulnerable countries

Refurbishing of Laptops

As shown in the e-waste analysis section, 7% of the e waste or about 37188 kg generated would be due to notebooks. According to what I have personally learned from my experience as an Intern at ePro Recyclers, a company that refurbishes laptops, notebooks as well as myriad other electronic devices, almost all these notebooks can be refurbished and resold at a value nearly 50% lower than the price at which it was originally sold. The total value of these notebooks when sold stands at $14,795,000. This means that approximately $7,397,500 can be retrieved annually, promoting a circular economy, if all of these notebooks are refurbished. Currently, 12.5% of this waste is recycled in the US. Hence, the lost value is calculated to be about $6,472,812.

Below is a set of detailed instructions on how one could refurbish a laptop by oneself. The paraphrenalia (if any) is mentioned step-wise. All information and instructions are from my three-week long internship.

    Firstly, Check for external damage

  • Most laptops and desktops have external damage and are therefore sent for replacement. If this damage isn’t significant, it can be ignored. Scratches and cracks on the Bezel of laptops are most common.

    If the laptop doesn’t turn on

    If the laptop does not turn on, check the power cables first as the laptops’ batteries may have simply run out of charge as the power cables stopped working.
  • One could check the power cable with the help of a multimeter. Place one lead of the multimeter on each of the prongs and the other on the console as a check for connectivity. If the power cable isn’t in working condition, replace the cable and try charging the laptop.
  • If one doesn’t have a multimeter, simply use another power cable and try charging the laptop and turning it on after charging it a little. If the laptop doesn’t turn on still, the cable is most likely okay. To confirm that the cable is perfectly okay as it could be a case where both the laptop and the power cable aren’t working, try connecting the power cable to another laptop that’s in working condition. If there’s no issue with the power cables, inspect the laptop for any physical factors such as the spilling of water, which may have caused damage to the circuitry inside the laptop.
  • If the water or any liquid was spilled on it recently, turn off the laptop immediately as it poses a risk.
    Then, close the laptop and flip it over. Use a screwdriver to open the back cover of the laptop. The back cover is called the base cover. Open the laptop, disconnect the battery, remove all the parts and dry them thoroughly (Instructions to open the laptop at the end). Reassemble all parts and try booting the laptop.
    If there’s either no visible physical damage or the laptop doesn’t boot even after the drying
  • Open the base cover, the battery is mostly the largest and the first thing one sees. If one notices a bulge in the battery, disconnect it and replace the battery immediately as it poses a fire hazard.
  • If there’s no bulge, test the battery by plugging in a multimeter on either terminal and check if the voltage is as mentioned on the battery. One has to connect to the ground pin of the battery before plugging the multimeter into the terminals. One could find the ground pin location online or use a multimeter to figure it out (the ground pin has resistance → 0 as in, the resistance is very close to 0 Ohms). If found to be far lower, the battery must be changed.
    Then check the connections to both the battery and the screen. If they haven’t been connected properly, one could connect it oneself. If the wires have been damaged or if the pins have been damaged, try replacing the wire or pin.

    If the laptop still doesn’t boot:

  • Open the laptop and take out the motherboard. Search the motherboard for parts or areas that have been fried or that have suffered damage. If the motherboard has been damaged in any visible way, one has to replace the motherboard.
  • Check if the heat sink is working properly by seeing if there’s any visible damage to the heat sink.
  • Check the processing chip as well: if any of the pins of the chip are bent, replace the chip.

    If the laptop still doesn’t boot:

      If one can source parts of their laptop, open the laptop (Instructions to open the laptop at the end), disconnect the battery and remove the RAM of the laptop (if possible). The RAM is pivotal in booting the laptop and showing the GUI to the users hence, with a dysfunctional RAM, the laptop doesn’t turn on. Replace the RAM with an identical one.
      If this also doesn’t work, one has mostly tried most possibilities, the problem may be due to a very small issue in the motherboard or elsewhere that is very hard for a user to detect. It would be best if one disposed of the laptop.

      Malfunction in a laptop part

      If a certain part of the laptop fails to function properly, these problems are fortunately and usually associated with peripherals and are easy to solve. For poor battery life:
      • Boot the laptop and try accessing the BIOS. The methods to access this vary depending on the manufacturer however, for my laptop, I had to press the F12 key immediately after the laptop booted. one could search for this on the internet, it’s easily available.
      • After accessing the BIOS, one can navigate with the help of a navigation panel and view a whole host of things. Look out for battery health: replace if needed.
    For other parts, one could test them individually by searching on the internet, if the part fails to work, one’ll have to replace that part alone. The manufacturer would have specified the make, type and all necessary details for one to identify the replacement. If the laptop doesn’t connect to the Wifi or isn’t able to access the internet, one’ll have to open the laptop, disconnect the battery and replace the Wifi card.

    Opening a laptop

    First, identify the screws that one would be dealing with by looking at the ones used in the base cover. Accordingly, pick out the tools and remove the screws of the base cover.
    After that, disconnect the battery by gently pulling out the power cord. Be careful as one may end up damaging the pins. Remove the battery and then proceed to remove the other parts after.
    At the first layer, one would most likely have to remove the Wifi card, battery and the RAM. Then, find the cord connecting the keyboard and remove that as well. Each laptop’s structure is different but it's the same drill: disconnect, unscrew and remove.
    While putting it all back together, one will have to put it back in the exact opposite order: place the component; put the screws and connect the cords.

    A few points to remember

  • There are some companies and brands that, in the name of integrating components, have an in-built RAM, Wifi card and everything else. Some even have screws that regular tools can’t remove. These are almost impossible to refurbish, the max one could do is to open the laptop and replace the entire motherboard and the parts embedded on it, which is almost as expensive as buying a new laptop. These companies and brands are hindering us in our collective fight against climate change and intentionally making their products hard to refurbish.
  • If any part is embedded onto the motherboard, do not attempt to remove it, it’s of no use. one has to change the entire motherboard
    If one is disposing of the laptop, please do so responsibly: either exchange it with the retailer of a laptop or put it in an e waste bin.

Battery Technology

While the refurbishing process I had learned about during my internship helped extend the life of devices, the same cannot be said about the components. As the internship progressed, it became increasingly clear. Dysfunctional components were simply replaced and disposed off. While components were replaced only when found to be dysfunctional, it was a common practice to replace the battery every time.

Most batteries used in laptops and other electronics are Lithium ion batteries specifically, Lithium Cobalt Oxide batteries. As their name suggests, these batteries primarily rely on Lithium and Cobalt. While the production of Lithium is extremely water-intensive, the extraction of Cobalt from countries such as DRC is riddled with human rights issues. Thus, I was appalled when I learned that a component that is produced at such an enormous cost is simply discarded - I wanted to work on something that extended the life of batteries.

    I got an opportunity to intern at The Energy Company, a battery-tech start-up. I was assigned to work on the Flexitwin, their flagship SaaS product, which is focused on extending the life of battery packs.
    While I was a data science intern here, I had to incorporate domain knowledge for building various solutions. I started by porting a regression model built in MATLAB, used to predit the State-of-Charge (SOC). I made the model more accessible with comparable performance by using Julia. Later, I improved the accuracy with hyperparameter tuning and open-sourced the code on Github, available here.
    The model used the following quantities as inputs:
  • Voltage (measured cell terminal voltage, sense leads welded directly to battery terminal)
  • Current (measure current in amps)
  • Ah (measured amp-hours, with Ah counter typically reset after each charge, test, or drive cycle)
  • Wh (measured watt-hours, with Wh counter reset after each charge, test, or drive cycle)
  • Battery_Temp_degC (battery case temperature, at middle of battery, in degrees Celsius measured with a AD592 +/-1degC accuracy temperature sensor)
